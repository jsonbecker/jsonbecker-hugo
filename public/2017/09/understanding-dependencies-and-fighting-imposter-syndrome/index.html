Understanding Dependencies and Fighting Imposter Syndrome

September 20, 2017
∞

What is this post/project?

This is the first in what I hope will be a series of blog posts tackling imposter syndrome.

After 6 years of writing R code, I still lack confidence reading the source of many of the packages that I rely on. At least part of this is a total lack of experience with C++, an increasingly important part of writing performant R packages. But I think mostly I just don’t make the time to dive deep. I am intimidated, and the code I write works for what I need to accomplish.

The last three years I have been writing my own R packages internally at Allovue. This experience has made me a much better R programmer and a better software developer in general. I can still remember feeling like writing more than a series of hacky scripts felt daunting, yet here I am writing my own packages that make me proud.

So I decided to embark on a project. I am going to fight my imposter syndrome, and hopefully yours, by going through a key package and making sense of it in public. I will be exposing how I read R code, which I bet will help some beginners tackle larger code bases than they are used to working with. I will expose what I need to learn, and how I go about learning it. Hopefully, the process will help me learn how to be a better reader of R code and how to be a part of larger open source projects and take a few readers along with me.

Package Choice

I may change what package I choose to use for this series of posts. But for now, I’ve chosen dbplyr. I thought this was a good candidate package because:

  1. I use tidyverse packages every day.
  2. I use databases every day, and write as much SQL as R. I feel like I understand most of the background for the problem space that dbplyr seeks to address.
  3. dplyr and tidyr feel overly intimidating, versus a component to make those packages work with databases. I may find this is a deep error which pushes me more into dplyr internals. This will either be a happy accident or drive me somewhere else.
